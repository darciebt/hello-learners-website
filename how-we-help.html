Understanding learning differences

What is a specific learning disorder?

A specific learning disorder (sometimes called a learning disability, learning difference, or SLD) is a brain-based difference in how a child processes certain kinds of information. The most well-known are dyslexia (reading), dyscalculia (numbers), and dysgraphia (writing) — but they often overlap, and they frequently sit alongside differences in how the child processes sounds, language, or visual information.

What they all have in common: the child is usually bright and working hard, but something specific in the way their brain handles the task is getting in the way. That means the fix isn't "more practice" or "try harder." It's targeted teaching that matches how their brain actually learns.

Learning Differences

Dyslexia
Reading · Spelling · Phonological processing

A learning difference in how the brain connects the sounds of language to letters on the page. Children with dyslexia are typically bright and hardworking, but reading, spelling, and writing feel much harder than they should. The most common specific learning disorder we see at Hello Learners.

arrow_forward
Developmental Language Disorder
Spoken language · Comprehension · DLD

A persistent difficulty understanding and using spoken language that can't be explained by another condition. DLD affects roughly 1 in 14 children. It often flies under the radar because these kids can seem quiet, shy, or inattentive — when really, they're missing the language behind the instructions.

arrow_forward
Dysgraphia
Writing · Spelling · Fine motor

A learning difference that affects writing — handwriting that's slow, effortful, or hard to read; spelling that doesn't match what the child can say out loud; and the exhausting experience of knowing what you want to write but being unable to get it on the page.

arrow_forward
Dyscalculia
Numbers · Maths · Logic

A specific learning disorder that affects number sense and mathematical reasoning. Children with dyscalculia can struggle to estimate quantities, remember number facts, or follow the steps of a maths problem — even when they're doing well in other areas.

arrow_forward
Auditory Processing Disorder
Hearing · Phonological awareness

A difference in how the brain interprets what the ears hear. Children can struggle to tell apart similar sounds in words (ship vs chip), follow instructions in noisy classrooms, or keep up when several people are talking — even though their hearing is fine.

arrow_forward
Visual Processing Disorder
Visual perception · Reading

A difference in how the brain makes sense of information taken in through the eyes. Letters can appear to jump, reverse, or blur on the page, making reading, spelling, and copying from the board much harder — even with perfect eyesight.
